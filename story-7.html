Eleanor Hughes

One of the first female executives in national magazine publishing, Eleanor talks about breaking formidable glass ceilings in the 1960s with a formidable work ethic, a sharp red pencil, and a sharper wit.

Listen to this story narrated by the author

If you wanted to terrify a junior copywriter in the Madison Avenue boom of the late 1960s, you simply told them Eleanor Hughes was reviewing their manuscript. "I wasn't mean," Eleanor clarifies, sipping her Earl Grey tea. "I was precise. But men in those days routinely confused female competence with hostility."

Eleanor entered the publishing industry not from an elite Ivy League communications program, but from the typing pool. Following a brief stent as a codebreaker during the tail-end of World War II, she relocated to New York City and accepted a job as a secretary at a major literary magazine. The unspoken rule was clear: women typed the words, men chose them.

"I noticed the editors were frequently drunk by 2 PM on a Tuesday," she says dryly. "And their spelling was atrocious. So, I started quietly rewriting the copy before it went to print. Eventually, the editor-in-chief summoned me. I thought I was being fired for insubordination. Instead, he made me a junior editor. He said he liked my 'masculine' clarity. I decided not to correct him."

Her ascent to Senior Editor and eventually Managing Director was marked by relentless battles. She had to fight for equal pay, she fought to be allowed in executive meetings held in "men only" clubs, and she fought the expectation that her career was merely a placeholder until she got married.

"You cannot apologize for taking up space. If you are competent, you belong in the room. Make them deal with your reality."

Eleanor's greatest triumph wasn't just the title on her door; it was the door she wedged open for the women who followed. She deliberately hired female writers, promoted female editors, and challenged the patriarchal narratives dominating the stories they published.

At ninety-nine years old, her mind remains as sharp as the red editor's pencil she wielded for decades. Her advice for young professionals today? "Stop agonizing over whether people like you. Likability is a trap designed to keep you compliant. Focus on whether you respect yourself. If your work is excellent, their fondness is irrelevant."
