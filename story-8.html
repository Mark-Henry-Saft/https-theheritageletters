Mateo Rivera

A retired deep-sea fisherman recounts the terrifying majesty of a hurricane at sea, the profound unpredictability of the ocean, and the calm that miraculously follows the hardest storms.

Listen to this story narrated by the author

If you stare at the horizon long enough, Mateo Rivera says, it stops being a straight line and starts becoming a promise or a threat. For forty-five years, that horizon was both his paycheck and his daily reality. Mateo was a third-generation deep-sea fisherman, wrestling fifty-pound swordfish and violent weather from the deck of a commercial trawler.

Raised in coastal San Juan, Mateo started working on his uncle’s boat when he was fifteen. The sea was an unforgiving mentor. It taught him mathematics, meteorology, and an unrelenting physical endurance.

mateo Youth

"We didn't have GPS or advanced sonar back then," Mateo recalls, his skin weathered into a roadmap of sun exposure and salt spray. "You navigated by reading the color of the water, the shape of the clouds, the feel of the deck under your boots. You respected the ocean, because the ocean absolutely did not care about you."

That respect was solidified in September of 1978, when a rogue storm off the coast of Florida caught his crew entirely off guard. For thirty hours, fifty-foot swells pitched the seventy-foot vessel like a child’s toy. They lost their radio, half their bait, and nearly lost two deckhands.

"When the storm is breaking over the bow, praying won't save you. Fastening the hatch will save you. Panic is a luxury you cannot afford."

Mateo considers surviving that night his greatest triumph. Not for the feat of endurance, but for the profound shift in perspective it gave him when morning finally arrived. The sea eventually settled into an unnatural, glassy calm. The silence after a tempest, he insists, is the closest a man can feel to touching something holy.

As he watches the harbor traffic today from his small cottage porch, his advice to modern generations is born from that experience. "People spend their lives trying to avoid a storm," he shakes his head softly. "But storms are inevitable. Instead of running from the rain, learn how to build a stronger boat."
