Iris Johansen

A prolific botanist who spent her life studying rare orchids explains what observing extremely slow plant growth taught her about patience, adaptability, and human relationships.

Listen to this story narrated by the author

Orchids are notoriously temperamental. They demand precise humidity, exact light angles, and very specific soil compositions. According to Dr. Iris Johansen, who spent thirty-five years classifying epiphytes for a major botanical institute, they are remarkably like people.

Iris grew up in post-war Sweden where winters were long, dark, and demanding. Her fascination with biology started in a tiny, heated greenhouse her grandfather built to force tulips before spring. To young Iris, the ability to manipulate the environment to coax life from a dormant bulb was nothing short of magic.

iris Youth

"I learned early on that you cannot command a plant to throw a bloom," she says, her hands resting elegantly on a stack of heavily annotated field journals. "You can only provide the conditions over a very long period, and then you must wait. Sometimes for years. Modern society hates waiting."

Her career highlights involved months-long expeditions into the humid cloud forests of Costa Rica and the dense jungles of Borneo, searching for unrecorded species. The work was grueling, solitary, and required an intense, hyper-focused form of observation. Iris often spent twelve hours crouched in the mud, documenting the minuscule progress of an opening bud.

"The greatest mistake we make with both plants and people is trying to force them to conform to our schedule. Growth happens on its own timeline."

Her greatest triumph, however, was successfully propagating the Ghost Orchid from seed in captivity—a process that took nearly fifteen years of trial and error, modifying microscopic fungi ratios in a sterile lab. She refuses to take credit for "creating" the bloom. "I just eventually stopped getting in the orchid's way," she laughs.

Living now in a sun-drenched cottage surrounded by ferns she speaks to daily, her advice is a gentle correction to a fast-paced world. "We expect relationships, careers, and healing to happen instantly. We buy fertilizer hoping to speed things up. It doesn't work. The work is in the waiting. Have the patience to let your life unfold."
